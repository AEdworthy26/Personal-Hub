The Capital Stack: How Development Projects Are Funded

Development projects are rarely funded from a single source. Instead, finance is structured in layers — each with a different position in the risk hierarchy, a different cost, and different rights if the scheme fails. This layered structure is called the capital stack.

Understanding the capital stack is not just a theoretical exercise. As a Planning and Development surveyor, you will encounter it constantly: when advising clients on scheme viability, when reviewing a development appraisal for a lender, or when explaining to a client why their return looks different depending on how the deal is financed.

Key Term — Capital Stack

The capital stack is the full set of funding sources used to finance a development, arranged in order of seniority. Each layer has a different risk profile, cost, and repayment priority. In a default, the most senior layer is repaid first; equity — at the top — is last.

Layer 1: Senior Debt

Senior debt is the primary and largest source of development funding, typically accounting for 55–70% of total project costs. It is provided by clearing banks (Barclays, Lloyds, NatWest), challenger banks, or specialist development lenders such as OakNorth or Octopus Real Estate.

Lenders use two key metrics to determine how much they will lend:

  • Loan to Cost (LTC): The loan as a percentage of total development costs (land + build + fees). Typically capped at 65–70%. This ensures the developer has meaningful “skin in the game” and that cost overruns don’t immediately threaten the lender’s position.
  • Loan to GDV: The loan as a percentage of the completed scheme’s Gross Development Value. Typically capped at 60–65%. This is a backstop: it prevents the lender advancing too much relative to what the asset will be worth on completion. On a thin-margin scheme, this cap can become the binding constraint.
APC Tip — Worked Example

A developer builds 20 townhouses at a total cost of £8m. GDV is estimated at £12m.

Max LTC (65%): £8m × 65% = £5.2m
Max against GDV (60%): £12m × 60% = £7.2m
The LTC cap is the binding constraint. Maximum senior debt = £5.2m. The remaining £2.8m must come from mezzanine or equity. Be ready to walk an examiner through a calculation like this.

How drawdowns work: Funds are not released in a lump sum. Drawdowns are tied to construction milestones (e.g. completion of foundations, watertight shell, practical completion). Before each release, the lender’s appointed monitoring surveyor — typically a quantity surveyor — certifies that the stated work has been completed to the required standard. Their fees are charged to the borrower.

Interest: Charged on drawn (not undrawn) funds. Most development finance is now priced against SONIA (Sterling Overnight Index Average) plus a margin of typically 2–4%, depending on the lender, scheme complexity and borrower track record. Interest is almost always rolled up — added to the loan balance rather than paid monthly — to protect the developer’s cashflow during the build. This rolled-up interest compounds over time and must be accurately modelled in the appraisal.

Security: The lender takes a first legal charge over the site. If the developer defaults, the lender can appoint a receiver and sell the site or completed units to recover their debt.

Layer 2: Mezzanine Finance

Mezzanine finance fills the gap between what the senior lender will advance and what the developer has available in equity. It sits above senior debt in the capital stack — meaning it is repaid after senior debt in a default scenario — and below equity.

  • Who provides it: Specialist mezzanine lenders and property finance funds. Rarely offered by high-street banks.
  • Cost: Significantly higher than senior debt — typically 12–20% per annum, reflecting the higher risk of loss. Sometimes structured as a combination of interest and a profit share arrangement.
  • Leverage effect: Adding mezzanine can push total debt to 80–90% of total costs, reducing the developer’s equity requirement substantially.

Why a developer would use it: Consider the same £8m scheme above. Without mezzanine, the developer needs £2.8m of their own equity. If they bring in a mezzanine lender to cover £1.6m of that gap (bringing total debt to £6.8m, or 85% LTC), the developer’s equity requirement drops to just £1.2m. The same absolute profit now represents a much higher return on the smaller equity outlay — but the risk is equally amplified, since all debt must be repaid before the developer sees any return.

Watch Out — Mezzanine Is Not Free Money

At 15% per annum on £1.6m, mezzanine costs £240,000 per year. On an 18-month build programme that’s £360,000 in finance costs. If the scheme runs three months over programme, add another £60,000. On a marginal scheme, this erodes developer profit quickly and can tip a viable appraisal into loss. Always model mezzanine costs carefully and stress-test for programme overrun.

Layer 3: Equity

Equity is the developer’s own capital, or capital contributed by a joint venture (JV) partner. It occupies the top of the capital stack: highest risk, last to be repaid, but with the greatest potential upside.

Why equity is expensive: Equity investors (including the developer themselves) are the first to lose money if a scheme underperforms. There is no security against their contribution, no fixed rate of return, and no obligation to repay before other creditors. In exchange for carrying this risk, they are entitled to the developer’s profit — which is why developers typically require a minimum return of 15–20% profit on cost (or 12–15% profit on GDV) to justify committing equity to a scheme.

Joint venture equity and the waterfall: On larger or more complex schemes, developers often bring in a funding partner — an institution, private equity fund, or high-net-worth investor — who contributes equity in exchange for a share of the profit. The terms are governed by a waterfall mechanism, which sets out in what order and in what proportions profits are distributed once debt has been repaid.

Key Term — Equity Waterfall

A waterfall defines how returns flow between equity parties. A typical two-tier structure: (1) return of all contributed capital to both parties; (2) a preferred return to the funding partner (e.g. 8% IRR); (3) surplus profits split at an agreed ratio, often 70/30 (funder/developer). The developer benefits disproportionately from outperformance above the hurdle rate — this share of upside profit is called the promote.

Why This Matters for Your APC

As a P&D surveyor, you will regularly encounter development finance in practice. You do not need to be a lender or a finance specialist, but you need to speak the language fluently and understand the implications:

  • Viability assessments: Finance costs are a key input in any development appraisal. Getting the interest calculation wrong — or forgetting to model rolled-up interest compounding — can materially distort the result. A surveyor who understands how debt is structured produces more credible appraisals.
  • Advising clients on deal structure: A client asking “should we use mezzanine?” is asking you to weigh the cost of finance against the reduction in equity and the amplified return. That is a judgement call that requires understanding all three layers.
  • Working with lenders: Whether advising a borrower or acting as monitoring surveyor, understanding what a lender is looking for — LTC, LTV, drawdown conditions — makes you a more effective intermediary and a more credible professional.
  • APC interviews: Examiners frequently ask candidates to explain how a specific scheme is financed, or to identify a finance cost error in an appraisal. Being able to articulate the capital stack clearly is a mark of Level 3 competence in Project Finance.

Summary

What to Take Away

Key Points
  • The capital stack layers finance by risk: senior debt (lowest risk, repaid first) → mezzanine → equity (highest risk, last to receive any return).
  • Senior debt is typically capped at 65% LTC or 60% of GDV — whichever is lower. Interest is usually rolled up and must be modelled carefully in any appraisal.
  • Mezzanine fills the funding gap between senior debt and equity. It is expensive (12–20% p.a.) and only appropriate where deal economics are strong enough to absorb the cost.
  • Equity bears residual risk. Developer profit (typically 15–20% on cost) is the return on equity — which is why this benchmark is used as a viability test.
  • Lenders appoint a monitoring surveyor to certify drawdowns independently. This is a common professional role in P&D practice and worth understanding in detail for your APC.
  • Finance costs — including rolled-up interest and fees — can materially erode developer profit. Always model programme risk and stress-test finance costs in sensitivity analysis.

Project Finance sits at Level 3 of the P&D pathway, meaning the APC expects you to go beyond knowing what these structures are — you should be able to advise a client on the implications of different financing approaches, identify errors in a finance model, and explain how the capital stack affects scheme viability.

Q&A

Test Your Knowledge

What is the difference between Loan to Cost (LTC) and Loan to GDV, and which typically acts as the binding constraint?
LTC measures the loan as a % of total development costs. Loan to GDV measures the loan against the completed scheme’s estimated value. LTC is typically the binding constraint on straightforward residential schemes (where cost certainty is higher). Loan to GDV acts as a backstop: on a thin-margin scheme where GDV is relatively low vs costs, the GDV cap can become the tighter of the two.
A developer has total costs of £5m and a GDV of £8m. What is the maximum senior debt at 65% LTC and 60% of GDV? Which is the binding constraint?
LTC: £5m × 65% = £3.25m. Loan to GDV: £8m × 60% = £4.8m. The LTC cap of £3.25m is the binding constraint. The developer must fund the remaining £1.75m from mezzanine and/or their own equity.
Why is development finance interest usually “rolled up” and what is the key risk of this approach?
During construction there is typically no income — units haven’t been sold or let. Rolling up interest avoids requiring the developer to service debt from their own funds during the build, protecting cashflow. The risk is that interest compounds: the total bill is higher than a simple flat rate suggests. If the scheme runs over programme, rolled-up interest grows significantly — which is why programme risk is one of the most important sensitivity variables to stress-test.
Who appoints the monitoring surveyor and what is their role?
The monitoring surveyor is appointed by the lender (not the developer) to provide independent oversight of the project on the lender’s behalf. They review the budget, programme and consultant appointments before first drawdown, then certify each subsequent drawdown by confirming construction work has been completed to the requisite standard. They report cost overruns and programme slippage to the lender. Their fees are charged to the borrower as a project cost.
A developer tells you mezzanine finance will “boost their returns.” Explain the mechanism and identify the key risk.
Mezzanine reduces the developer’s equity contribution while keeping absolute profit the same — the same £1m profit on £1m equity (vs £2m equity) doubles the return on equity. The key risk: mezzanine costs 12–20% p.a. and ranks above equity. If costs overrun or GDV falls, mezzanine interest erodes profit quickly and the developer’s equity is wiped out first. Leverage amplifies both gains and losses.
What is an equity waterfall and why does the “promote” matter to a developer?
A waterfall sets out how profits are distributed between equity parties once the project completes. A typical structure: (1) return of all capital; (2) preferred return to the funding partner; (3) surplus profits split at an agreed ratio (e.g. 70/30). The promote is the developer’s disproportionate share of profits above the hurdle rate — it incentivises outperformance, since the developer keeps a larger share of any upside beyond the funder’s minimum return.

As high-street banks have tightened risk appetite, challenger and specialist lenders have grown their market share significantly. Institutional Debt Transforms BTR Finance Structures

Insurance companies and pension funds are increasingly providing long-term “exit finance” for Build-to-Rent schemes at margins of sub-3%, refinancing the short-term construction loan on practical completion. Understanding this refinancing step is now a core part of advising on BTR viability.
